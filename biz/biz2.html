Transfer Pricing: How Profits Move Across Borders

What Is Transfer Pricing?

[Disclaimer: This example is simplified for educational discussion purposes.]

Transfer pricing refers to the prices charged for transactions between related companies within the same multinational group.

While total group profit stays the same, where the profit is reported can change — and that directly affects how much tax is paid.

Why Tax Rates Matter

Multinational companies often operate in countries with very different corporate tax rates. This creates incentives to shift profits from high-tax countries to low-tax countries.

Numerical Example: Avoiding a High Tax Rate

The example below shows how transfer pricing can reduce the group’s total tax burden without changing real business activity.

✔ Total group profit is the same (USD 400M), but total tax drops from USD 110M to USD 80M by shifting profit to the low-tax country.

The Arm’s Length Rule

Tax authorities require transfer prices to follow the arm’s length principle — prices should reflect what independent companies would agree on.

If prices are artificially set to avoid tax, authorities may impose adjustments, penalties, and interest.
