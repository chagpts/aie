Stripe Payment Intents API — Accounting-Focused Overview

This article explains Stripe payment events through the lens of IFRS 15 and US GAAP, with a focus on revenue recognition.

IFRS 15: Five-Step Revenue Recognition Model

Step 1: Identify the Contract

A contract exists when enforceable rights and obligations arise. This is usually established at order confirmation — not when a payment flow starts.

Step 2: Identify Performance Obligations

Each distinct good or service must be identified. Most Stripe use cases involve a single obligation satisfied at a point in time.

Step 3: Determine the Transaction Price

The transaction price is the amount expected to be received. Stripe fees and refunds affect presentation, not the transaction price itself.

Step 4: Allocate the Transaction Price

If multiple obligations exist, the price is allocated based on relative stand-alone selling prices.

Step 5: Recognize Revenue

Revenue is recognized when control transfers to the customer. This typically aligns with charge.created or charge.succeeded, not Stripe balance availability.

Key takeaway:
Revenue follows economic substance, not cash movement.

Stripe API Objects Often Confused with Revenue

Stripe Object Meaning Revenue? Reason
payment_intent.succeeded Payment flow completed ❌ No settlement guarantee
balance.available Withdrawable funds ❌ Cash timing only
charge.created Legal charge established ⚠️ Authorization risk remains
charge.succeeded Approved and settled ✅ Enforceable right exists

Internal Discussion: Revenue Timing

Manager: Why are we recognizing revenue based on balance.available?

Staff: Because that’s when cash becomes available in Stripe.

Manager: Cash availability does not define revenue under IFRS 15.

Staff: Then what timing is more appropriate?

Manager: Revenue aligns more closely with charge.created or charge.succeeded, when the right to consideration exists.

One-Line Accounting Map

Revenue → charge.succeeded
Fees    → balance_transaction (fee)
Cash    → payout.paid
