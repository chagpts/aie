Merchant Cash Advance (MCA): Accounting & Audit Perspective

Reference:
https://stripe.com/resources/more/merchant-cash-advance

This study material explains Merchant Cash Advance (MCA) using Stripe as an example, from a US GAAP accounting, audit, and regulatory perspective.

MCA US GAAP Audit Judgment Financing vs Loan

1. What Is a Merchant Cash Advance?

A Merchant Cash Advance (MCA) is not structured as a traditional loan. Instead, the provider purchases a portion of the merchant’s future receivables in exchange for an upfront cash payment.

  • No stated interest rate
  • Repayment is tied to actual sales volume
  • No fixed maturity date
  • Payments fluctuate with daily sales

2. MCA vs. Factoring vs. Loan

Item MCA Factoring Loan
Underlying asset Future sales Existing A/R Cash
Repayment certainty Variable Mostly fixed Fixed
Interest rate None stated Discount implied Explicit
GAAP classification Financing arrangement Sale or secured borrowing Debt

3. Is MCA a Loan Under US GAAP?

Under US GAAP, MCA is generally not accounted for as a loan if the substance of the arrangement shows:

  • No unconditional repayment obligation
  • Provider assumes variability risk of sales
  • No fixed or determinable maturity
Key audit concept:
Auditors assess substance over form. Even without the word “loan,” if repayment is effectively fixed, MCA may be recharacterized as debt.

4. Debate Topic: Loan Regulation vs. MCA

Finance Team Lead Position
Loans are heavily regulated by usury laws and lending regulations. MCA is a purchase of receivables, not a loan, so those regulations shouldn’t apply.

Auditor Challenge
Regulation aside, we care about accounting substance. If repayment is practically guaranteed, it may still be debt under GAAP.

Conclusion GAAP View
Regulatory classification and accounting classification are different. MCA may avoid loan regulation, yet still fail debt classification—or vice versa.

5. Does MCA Create Financing Cost?

Yes. Even without stated interest, MCA embeds an implied financing cost. The difference between cash received and total expected remittance represents the cost of capital.

  • No interest expense line item
  • Recognized through effective yield over collections
  • Presented as financing cash flow impact
