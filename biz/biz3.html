Cost Sharing Arrangements: Sharing Costs, Sharing Value

What Is a Cost Sharing Arrangement?

[Disclaimer: This example is simplified for discussion and learning purposes.]

A Cost Sharing Arrangement (CSA), also called a Cost Contribution Arrangement (CCA), is an agreement where related companies agree to share the costs and risks of developing assets such as technology, software, or intellectual property.

In return, each participant receives the right to use the developed asset in its own market or territory.

Why Companies Use Cost Sharing

Developing intangible assets is expensive and risky. By sharing costs, multinational groups can:

  • Reduce individual financial burden
  • Align costs with expected benefits
  • Avoid duplicating R&D efforts
  • Support global expansion efficiently

✔ Contributions must reflect expected benefits — not legal ownership. Tax authorities closely review valuation and allocation methods.

Key Tax Concerns

Cost sharing arrangements are often audited because intangibles are difficult to value. Authorities focus on whether:

  • Contributions reflect realistic expected benefits
  • Participants control and bear relevant risks
  • Buy-in and buy-out payments are properly valued

Discussion Questions

  • How do companies estimate “expected benefits”?
  • Is cost sharing fair if one party controls the IP?
  • Why are intangibles harder to price than products?
  • Should tax authorities be stricter with CSAs?
  • Would you join a CSA if future benefits were uncertain?
