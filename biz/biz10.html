ITGC Failure Case

Topic: Why are ITGCs important for accounting?
Scenario: Discussion about ITGC failures during an audit.

Conversation

Alex (IT Auditor): During our audit, we found several ITGC issues.
Sarah (Finance Manager): Issues? But our accounting numbers look correct.
Alex: The numbers may look correct, but the controls behind them are weak.
Mike (System Developer): What kind of issues did you find?
Alex: First, many users share the same system ID.
Sarah: That’s for convenience. Is it really a problem?
Alex: Yes. If a journal entry is changed, we can’t identify who did it.
Mike: That also means there’s no accountability.
Sarah: Okay, what else?
Alex: There’s no formal change management. Program changes were made directly in the production system.
Mike: That’s risky. There’s no testing or approval record.
Sarah: Could that affect financial statements?
Alex: Absolutely. A small logic change can change revenue or expense calculations.
Mike: I also noticed there’s no regular backup test.
Alex: Exactly. Backups exist, but recovery was never tested.
Sarah: So even if we have backups, they might not work?
Alex: Yes. Untested backups are a common ITGC failure.
Sarah: What’s the audit impact of all this?
Alex: Because of these ITGC failures, we cannot rely on system-generated reports.
Mike: That means more manual testing for the audit.
Sarah: More work for us… and higher audit costs.
Alex: Correct. Weak ITGCs increase audit risk and audit effort.
Sarah: I get it now. ITGC failures don’t just affect IT — they affect the entire finance team.
Alex: Exactly. ITGCs are the foundation of reliable accounting.

Key Takeaways

  • Weak ITGCs increase audit risk.
  • System-generated data cannot be trusted without ITGCs.
  • Untested backups are a common ITGC failure.
