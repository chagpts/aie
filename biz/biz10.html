When ITGCs Fail — Why Accounting Numbers Can’t Be Trusted

Modern accounting relies heavily on systems. Journal entries, reports, and balances are often generated automatically. But what happens when the controls behind those systems fail?

1 What Are ITGCs?

IT General Controls (ITGCs) are basic system-level controls that support the reliability of accounting data. They do not calculate numbers — they protect the environment where numbers are created.

If ITGCs are weak, even perfectly designed accounting rules can produce unreliable results.

2 A Common ITGC Failure Scenario

During an audit, an IT auditor, a finance manager, and a system developer discuss several control issues found in the accounting system.

Issue Identified Why It Matters
Shared user IDs No way to identify who changed accounting data
No change management System logic can be altered without approval or testing
Untested backups Data recovery may fail during an incident

3 Why “The Numbers Look Fine” Is Not Enough

From a finance perspective, reports may appear accurate. However, auditors focus on how those numbers were created.

  • Who can access the system?
  • Who can change programs or settings?
  • Can data be recovered if something goes wrong?

Without clear answers, auditors cannot rely on system-generated reports.

4 Audit Impact of ITGC Failures

ITGC Weakness Audit Consequence
Weak access control Higher risk of unauthorized entries
No change controls Incorrect calculations may go undetected
Backup failures Incomplete accounting records

As a result, auditors must perform more manual testing, increasing both audit effort and cost.

5 The Key Lesson

ITGCs are not just an IT issue. They directly affect the credibility of financial statements.

Reliable accounting starts with reliable systems. Without ITGCs, accounting numbers become assumptions — not evidence.
