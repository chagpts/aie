When ITGCs Fail — Why Accounting Numbers Can’t Be Trusted

Modern accounting relies heavily on systems. Journal entries, reports, and balances are often generated automatically. But what happens when the controls behind those systems fail?

1 What Are ITGCs?

IT General Controls (ITGCs) are basic system-level controls that support the reliability of accounting data. They do not calculate numbers — they protect the environment where numbers are created.

If ITGCs are weak, even perfectly designed accounting rules can produce unreliable results.

2 A Common ITGC Failure Scenario

Issue Identified Why It Matters
Shared user IDs No accountability for data changes
No change management System logic can be modified without approval or testing
Untested backups Data recovery may fail when needed

3 Audit Impact

Even if reports look correct, auditors cannot rely on system-generated data when ITGCs are weak.

Weak ITGCs lead to higher audit risk, more manual testing, and increased audit cost.

Original Conversation (Role-play)

Alex (IT Auditor): During our audit, we found several ITGC issues.

Sarah (Finance Manager): Issues? But our accounting numbers look correct.

Alex: The numbers may look correct, but the controls behind them are weak.

Mike (System Developer): What kind of issues did you find?

Alex: First, many users share the same system ID.

Sarah: That’s for convenience. Is it really a problem?

Alex: Yes. If a journal entry is changed, we can’t identify who did it.

Mike: That also means there’s no accountability.

Alex: There’s no formal change management. Program changes were made directly in the production system.

Mike: That’s risky. There’s no testing or approval record.

Sarah: Could that affect financial statements?

Alex: Absolutely. A small logic change can change revenue or expense calculations.

Mike: I also noticed there’s no regular backup test.

Alex: Exactly. Untested backups are a common ITGC failure.

Sarah: So even if we have backups, they might not work?

Alex: Yes. Because of these ITGC failures, we cannot rely on system-generated reports.
